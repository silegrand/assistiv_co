The challenge

The system was not designed for the people who need it most.

Three and a half million older adults in the UK live independently but sit outside the reach of formal health and social care. They are not ill enough to qualify. Not yet dependent enough to be flagged. Invisible to existing systems until something goes wrong.

When people in this group try to access support, they encounter a fragmented landscape that demands navigation skills many no longer have. By the time they reach anyone who can help, the opportunity for early intervention has often passed.

For the individual

Living with uncertainty. Managing risk alone. One fall, one health event, one confused night and independence is gone. Not gradually, but suddenly.

For families and carers

Caught between constant worry and helplessness. Unable to monitor from a distance, uncertain when to act, carrying an invisible burden the system does not acknowledge.

For services

Avoidable crisis admissions. Delayed discharge. Residential placements that could have been prevented months earlier. The longer the system waits, the more expensive and complex the intervention becomes.

Our approach

Built around the person. Not the system's convenience.

Assistiv brings novel approaches and technologies to a population that conventional platforms have not been designed to reach. Every design decision is grounded in one principle: the person is always the author of what happens next.

01

A conversation, not an assessment

Assistiv begins with listening. A warm, unhurried conversation that surfaces what matters to the person, on their terms, in their language, without clinical framing. The technology is introduced only in direct response to what the person says they want.

02

Three independent perspectives

Assistiv synthesises what the person reports, what those close to them observe, and what the home environment signals. It brings these together monthly across multiple quality-of-life domains. Where the three perspectives diverge is where the signal that matters lives.

03

Connected to the care system

The platform is designed from the ground up to integrate with the NHS infrastructure that is coming, including the Single Patient Record and the shift toward neighbourhood-based, preventive care. The person holds the keys to what is shared and with whom.

Design principles

Privacy and dignity are not features. They are the architecture.

Every technology choice we make is filtered through a single question: does this serve the person's independence, or does it quietly undermine it? Assistiv is not surveillance. It is infrastructure that people choose, on their own terms, for their own reasons.

The platform is designed to be graduated. A person can begin with a conversation and nothing else. Technology, if any, is introduced incrementally, chosen by them, for reasons they have named, always reversible.

The person is always the author

Nothing is activated without explicit agreement. Nothing is shared without consent. The person controls what is monitored and who can see it. Assistiv follows where they lead.

No cameras. No wearables. No burden.

Assistiv is designed to be invisible in operation. Nothing to remember. Nothing to charge. Nothing that says "you are being watched." Passive by default, intrusive never.

Saying no is a valid outcome

A person who declines is not a failed engagement. They are a person exercising agency. The platform is designed to honour this at every step, without pressure or re-framing.

Data owned by the person

All processing happens within the home. Derived insights, not raw data, are the only thing that travels. The person holds the keys. Assistiv provides the infrastructure.

Outcomes

Earlier support. Measurable impact. Across the whole system.

Assistiv produces measurable outcomes at the level of the individual, their family, and the commissioning system. The Kent ICB pilot projection models £1.2m in system savings against £88k commissioning cost in the first quarter alone.

For the person

  • Lives longer at home, on their own terms
  • Earlier cognitive and physical support
  • Dignity and autonomy maintained
  • Meaningful activity sustained
  • Freedom from fear of falling undetected
  • Connection to family and community

For carers and families

  • Real-time awareness replaces constant worry
  • Family visits restored. No longer arriving as crisis managers
  • Carer wellbeing monitored and protected
  • Objective data for difficult family conversations
  • Reduced time spent on care coordination

For health and social care

  • Avoidable crisis admissions prevented
  • Safe rapid home discharge enabled

  • Population-level longitudinal data at scale
  • 14:1 return on investment at pilot scale
